Week 3 - Coaching Summary

24 September 2019

In his book Dialogue and the Art of Thinking Together, Bill Isaacs states: “we can influence and regenerate the inner ecology of human beings by transforming the quality of our conversations” (pg. 308). Even more directly, “the way we talk impacts the way we think.” A good coach is someone who encourages the kind of dialogue which helps a person regenerate their inner ecology. Through tactics like active listening, summarizing, and questioning, a coach is able to assist another person toward a better articulation of their thoughts and feelings. While it’s a deeply collaborative effort, the coach will likely spend most of the conversation using prompts to help their coachee work through something for which they haven’t quite found the words.

This process of “thinking together” can have a profound effect on the coachee, as Bill Isaacs describes it: “to help human beings to untie their own knots and liberate themselves” (pg. ix). This process includes helping a person reflect on their thoughts, discover the source of their ideas and uncover ways they might more fully express themselves in a truthful and articulate manner. It is a partnership in unraveling the mystery surrounding an individual and their thought process. This would then, in turn, alter the way the coachee sees the world and allow them to discover new ways of understanding their surroundings and other people.

For me, a great coach would help me to better understand the way I am perceived by, and interact with, other people. My occupation requires a good portion of my time to be filled with the creation and maintaining of human relationships, from members of my direct team to various stakeholders. It also includes customers and other end-users with whom I interact on a regular basis in order to understand their needs and concerns. Each of these interactions could be improved by transforming the quality of conversation and learning how to better engage in generative dialogue. We’re often navigating through complex spaces with potentially opposing goals, searching for some semblance of common ground. While I have been relatively successful in building relationships in the recent past, there is still plenty of opportunity to connect with others on a deeper, more human level.

Lastly, I believe a good coach is somebody who eventually makes themselves irrelevant. The process of supporting someone through the untying of their own knots and liberating themselves should include putting structures in place which no longer necessitate the further assistance of the coach. In fact, the coachee should be aware of what elements of the dialogue are helping them and how, so they can continue to improve while outside the safety net of a coach’s conversation. This process should lead to a regenerated inner ecology which can, in turn, reach out to and improve the inner ecology of other human beings. “Change in one part of that ecology can have impacts on the whole of the ecology, precisely because it is a living system” (pg. 308). The end-goal of the coach should be a positive shift in the ecology which goes on to affect the entire system.
